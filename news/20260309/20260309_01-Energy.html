Energy costs – how does our embedded network stack up?
Comparing Altogether Group rates
As part of the committee’s ongoing review of body corporate expenditure, we’ve conducted a detailed comparison of our current electricity arrangements against the retail plans available on the open market. The short answer: the embedded network is delivering genuine savings for every owner in the scheme.
This article sets out the findings and explains the context. An interactive cost comparison tool is also available – the link is at the end of this article.
How our electricity supply works
Our scheme operates an embedded electricity network managed by Altogether Group. Rather than each lot having a separate connection to the Energex grid, the body corporate purchases electricity in bulk through a single ‘gate meter’ at a wholesale rate. Altogether then meters and bills each lot individually based on actual usage.
This arrangement gives the scheme collective purchasing power that an individual household would not have. It also means our supply and usage rates are different from the standard retail plans you’d see advertised by energy companies.

It’s actually better than the table suggests
There is an important nuance that makes the embedded network even more competitive than the numbers above show. The retail plan rates listed are bundled rates – they include both energy charges and network distribution charges, because those plans are designed for customers connected directly to the Energex grid.
If an owner in our scheme were to switch to an external retailer, they would not pay the bundled rate. Instead, they would need to ask the retailer for an ‘energy only’ offer (covering just the electricity itself), and would continue paying network charges to the body corporate through Altogether at the regulated shadow price. That means two bills and a higher total cost than what the retail comparison websites show.
In short: the retail plans are already more expensive on paper – and the real cost of switching from inside the embedded network would be higher still.
Other costs and considerations if switching
Owners do have the legal right to choose an external retailer under Queensland’s ‘power of choice’ laws. However, there are practical costs and complications.
1. Metering
A new retailer may need to replace the Altogether meter with their own. This may come with a connection fee and a brief period without supply during the changeover.
2. Two bills
You would receive one bill from your new retailer for energy usage, and a separate bill from Altogether for network charges.
3. Retailer willingness
Not all retailers are willing to make offers to embedded network customers. The administrative complexity and low volume make it unattractive for some providers.
4. Cost borne by the owner
All opt-out costs sit with the individual owner. Exit fees are capped at $20 in Queensland, but metering and connection charges are additional and vary.
5. Returning is straightforward
If you switch and aren’t happy, you can return to the embedded network arrangement by contacting Altogether and your retailer. There is a 10-day cooling-off period on new contracts.

Summary
The embedded network arrangement through Altogether Group is delivering what it’s designed to deliver: lower electricity costs than owners would achieve on the open market. For a typical two-person household, the saving is at least $315 per year compared to the cheapest available fixed-rate retail plan – and the true saving is greater once the bundled-versus-energy-only rate difference is accounted for.
The committee will continue to monitor electricity costs as part of its regular reviews and will update owners if circumstances change.
